\section{Introduction}

Singapore University of Technology and Design (SUTD) has over 200 rooms split across 5 buildings.
Rooms are often communicated by name (e.g. ``Think Tank 1''),
and each room is assigned an address of the form
$$\texttt{x.y-z}$$
where $\texttt{x}$ is a building number,
$\texttt{y}$ is a floor number,
and $\texttt{z}$ is a room number.
Room address lookup---mapping rooms to their addresses---can be a hassle for staff, students, and visitors alike.
However, resources for this task remain limited.
Physical resources like campus signs exist,
but often require one to expend energy and time walking around.
Over the years,
digital resources like
the SUTD Assistive Maps (SAM) Telegram bot
\citep{sam}
have been developed.
However, they often fail on noisy input (e.g. strings with typos),
which can be especially common for
visitors unfamiliar with the rooms
and people with less experience typing in English.

To address this gap,
we introduce SUTD404,
a digital resource for room address lookup.
Visit
\href{https://github.com/gregorylimeurhen/sutd404}{github.com/gregorylimeurhen/sutd404}
for our code repository, and
\href{https://sutd404.vercel.app}{sutd404.vercel.app}
for our web application.
We trained on a T4 GPU for approximately 1 hour.

Let $V$ be a vocabulary,
and $R\subseteq V^{*}$ be a room set,
and $\prec$ be a total order on $R$,
and $L\in\N_{\geq 1}$ be a result count,
and $\prec_{L}$ be a total order on $R_{\prec}^{L}$,
and $A\subseteq V^{*}$ be an address set,
and $B\subseteq V$ be a boundary set,
and $C\in\N$ be a corruption count,
and $\alpha\in\R_{[0,1]}$ be a transposition rate,
and $\beta\in\R_{[0,1]}$ be a substitution rate,
and $\bm{\pi}=(\pi_\mathrm{train},\pi_\mathrm{val},\pi_\mathrm{test})\in\Delta_{C}^{2}$ be a training-validation-testing split,
and $I:V\rightharpoonup\Z^{2}$ be a keyboard-position map.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{$\textsc{Corrupt}(\bm{x})$}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\Require $\bm{x}\in V^{*}$
		\State ${
			(T_{L},T_{M},T_{R})
			\gets
			(V^{*}B\cup\{()\},(V\setminus B)^{+},BV^{*}\cup\{()\})
		}$
		\State ${
			T
			\gets
			\{
				\bm{u}\bm{w}b\bm{v}\bm{z}
				\mid
				\bm{u}\in T_{L},\,
				\bm{v},\bm{w}\in T_{M},\,
				\bm{z}\in T_{R},\,
				b\in B,\,
				\bm{x}=\bm{u}\bm{v}b\bm{w}\bm{z}
			\}
			\setminus
			\{\bm{x}\}
		}$
		\If{$T\neq\varnothing$}
			${\bm{x}\sim\alpha\,\mathcal{U}(T)+(1-\alpha)\,\delta(\bm{x})}$
		\EndIf
		\For{$i\in[1..\abs{\bm{x}}]$}
			\State ${
				S
				\gets
				\{
					k\in K
					\setminus
					\{x_{i}\}
					\mid
					I(k)-I(x_{i})
					\in
					[-1..1]
					\times
					[-2..2]
				\}
			}$
			\If{$S\neq\varnothing$}
				${x_{i}\sim\beta\,\mathcal{U}(S)+(1-\beta)\,\delta(x_{i})}$
			\EndIf
		\EndFor
		\State \Return $\bm{x}$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{$\textsc{Split}()$}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\State ${
			(D_\mathrm{train},D_\mathrm{val},D_\mathrm{test})
			\gets
			(\varnothing,\varnothing,\varnothing)
		}$
		\For{$r\in R$}
			\State $P\gets\varnothing$
			\While{$\abs{P}<C$}
				\State $r'\sim\textsc{Corrupt}(r)$
				\If{$r'\neq r$}
					$P\gets P\cup\{(r',r)\}$
				\EndIf
			\EndWhile
			\State ${
				P_\mathrm{train}
				\sim
				\mathcal{U}(\{Q\subseteq P\mid \abs{Q}=\pi_\mathrm{train}C\})
			}$
			\State ${
				P_\mathrm{val}
				\sim
				\mathcal{U}(\{Q\subseteq P\setminus P_\mathrm{train}\mid \abs{Q}=\pi_\mathrm{val}C\})
			}$
			\State ${
				P_\mathrm{test}
				\gets
				P\setminus(P_\mathrm{train}\cup P_\mathrm{val})
			}$
			\State ${
				(D_\mathrm{train},D_\mathrm{val},D_\mathrm{test})
				\gets
				(
					D_\mathrm{train}\cup P_\mathrm{train},
					D_\mathrm{val}\cup P_\mathrm{val},
					D_\mathrm{test}\cup P_\mathrm{test}
				)
			}$
		\EndFor
		\State \Return $(D_\mathrm{train},D_\mathrm{val},D_\mathrm{test})$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We generated corrupted strings
and split corrupted-uncorrupted room pairs
into training, validation, and testing sets.
Our corruption algorithm corrupts strings by transposing spans and substituting characters.
First, the algorithm samples whether to transpose spans by uniformly sampling from the space of span transpositions.
Then, for each character in the string, the algorithm samples whether to substitute the character by uniformly sampling from the space of character substitutions.
This space contains nearby characters collected by sliding a window of height $3$ and width $5$ centred at each position in $K$.
Finally, the algorithm returns the string.
The split is stratified by room to make the training, validation, and test sets comparable across rooms.
We set $C=50$, and $\alpha=0.5$, and $\beta=0.05$, and $\bm{\pi}=(0.8,0.1,0.1)$ to generate plausible-looking typos in a tractable way.

\newpage

We set $L=26$ because, grouping rooms by shared prefix up to terminal numbers,
the largest group (the group of Think Tank rooms) contains $26$ rooms,
so showing $26$ results could allow a user to find their room by prefix
without using terminal numbers for disambiguation.

\begin{definition}[Denoiser]
Let $f:V^{*}\times R\to\R$ be a scorer.
\begin{align*}
	\mathscr{D}_{f}
	&:
	V^{*}\to R
	\\
	\mathscr{D}_{f}(x)
	&=
	\min_{\prec}
	\argmax_{r\in R}
	f(x,r)
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

Our denoiser is a Nanochat-parameterised, character-level transformer of depth 2 with decoding constrained to the trie induced by $R$
\citep{nanochat}.
We use character-level tokenisation as typos are often character-level, and room names are often short enough for individual characters to be meaningful lexical units for our task.
We use trie-constrained decoding to efficiently avoid room hallucination.
Our denoiser is trained using a standard cross-entropy loss and AdamW optimiser.
A more complete list of hyperparameters can be found in our code repository.

For training and testing, we snapshot essential data and code files for each run into a portably compressed (e.g. ZIP) file for auditability.
On each epoch, we maintain the latest weights (i.e. the weights for that epoch) and best weights (i.e. the weights for the epoch with lowest validation loss) to ensure resumability for robustness to hardware instability.
We use early stopping to avoid spending compute after validation loss plateaus.
We log training losses, validation losses, and gradient norms to monitor fitting progress and stability.
We also log model answers and scores for convenient analysis.

\begin{definition}[Lookup]
$$
	\mathscr{L}:R\to A
$$
\end{definition}

Our lookup is a standard room-address table.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{$\textsc{Infer}(f,x)$}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\Require $f:V^{*}\times R\to\R$, $x\in V^{*}$
		\If{$x\not\in R$}
			$x\gets\mathscr{D}_{f}(x)$
		\EndIf
		\State $x\gets\mathscr{L}(x)$
		\State \Return $x$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Our inference algorithm receives a scorer and string as input.
Firstly, the algorithm denoises the string to a room using the scorer if the string is not a room.
Then, the algorithm looks up the address of the room.
Finally, the algorithm returns the address.

We conducted experiments for empirical validation of our denoiser and system.
Accuracy was measured as the percentage of test inputs for which the final predicted address equalled the gold address.
Top-$L$ accuracy was measured as the percentage of test inputs for which the gold address appeared among the final $L$ predicted addresses.
(Mean) latency was measured in milliseconds using the mean time taken to get an output from giving an input.
For fairness and reproducibility, we use a fixed seed and hardware.

We evaluated our denoiser against
substring Jaccard similarity (SJ),
negative Damerau--Levenshtein distance (DL),
and

Our denoiser outperformed the LCP, DL, and SJ denoisers by non-negligible margins,

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{$\textsc{Predict}(x)$}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\Require $x\in V^{*}$
		\State $x\gets\textsc{Strip}(x)$
		% example: " LT,2 " -> "LT,2"
		\State $x\gets\textsc{Depunctuate}(x)$
		% example: "LT,2" -> "LT2"
		\State $x\gets\textsc{Lowercase}(x)$
		% example: "LT2" -> "lt2"
		\State $x\gets\textsc{AlphanumericSplit}(x)$
		% example: "lt2" -> "lt 2"
		\State $x\gets\textsc{Alias}(x)$
		% example: "lt 2" -> "lecture theatre 2", read aliases.tsv to see my convention for this
		\If{$x\in R$}
			\Return $((x,\mathscr{L}(x)))$
		\EndIf
		\State ${
			\bm{f}
			\gets
			(
				\textsc{LCP}, % why/assumption: most frequent error type
				\textsc{SJ},
				\textsc{DL},
				\textsc{Ours} % why/assumption: least frequent error type
			)
		}$
		\For{$r\in R$}
			\For{$f\in\bm{f}$}
				\State ${
					p(r,f)
					\gets
					\abs{\{r'\in R\mid f(x,r')>f(x,r)\}}
					+
					1
				}$
			\EndFor
		\EndFor
		\For{$r\in R$}
			\State ${
				s(r)
				\gets
				\sum_{i=1}^{\abs{\bm{f}}}
				(1/\abs{R}^{i-1})
				(\abs{R}-p(r,f_{i}))
			}$
		\EndFor
		\State ${
			\bm{o}
			\gets
			\min_{\prec_{L}}
			\arg\max_{\bm{r}\in R_{\prec}^{L}}
			\sum_{i=1}^{L}
			s(r_{i})
		}$
		\State \Return $((o,\mathscr{L}(o))\mid o\in\bm{o})$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We implemented and deployed our system as a web application.
Our prediction algorithm receives a string as input.
Firstly, the algorithm normalises the string by
stripping whitespace,
removing punctuation,
lowercasing,
separating letters from numbers,
and replacing words with their aliases,
to make the string more closely match training strings.
Then, the algorithm computes top-ranked rooms using a Borda-style ranking.
We prioritise scorers by assumed error frequency,
placing prefix-sensitive signals first for more common errors
and using the trained denoiser for rarer errors.
Finally, the algorithm looks up the address of each room, returning top-ranked room-address pairs.

To further improve the user experience,
we also implemented the following features to make our web application more efficient and responsive.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Prefix caching.} We KV-cache previously computed prefix states and only process newly appended characters.
	\item \textbf{Incremental prediction.} We predict during typing rather than waiting for final submission.
	\item \textbf{Debounced querying.} We introduce a short debounce to avoid redundant intermediate inference on rapid keystrokes.
	\item \textbf{Bounded result caching.} We memoise recent solved queries while capping cache growth to prevent performance degradation over time.
	\item \textbf{Redundant-query suppression.} We skip empty, already displayed, and exact-match queries when full recomputation is unnecessary.
\end{itemize}

Our results suggest that SAM can be effective on mildly corrupted inputs with strong lexical overlap---occasionally recovering the exact target---but degrades markedly as noise becomes more severe.
We conclude that SAM can be robust to limited orthographic variation,
but can also fail to generalise beyond surface-form similarity.
Our system convincingly outperformed SAM, achieving complete recovery.
Overall, we believe these results demonstrate strong promise
and provide practical foundations for reliable campus assistance.
